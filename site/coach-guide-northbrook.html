Northbrook Jr High Meet

Notes and prep for coaches heading into the Northbrook Jr High meet. This is a great meet run by welcoming people — low pressure, high fun. Read through before the meet and brief your athletes and parent volunteers accordingly.

Meet Overview

What to expect and how to set the tone for your athletes.

  • This is a local, informal meet run by genuinely great people. The vibe is welcoming, not cutthroat. Coaches from other schools will help each other out.
  • No registration — athletes just show up and run. There’s no entry limit, so every athlete who wants to compete in an event can. Let them try things.
  • Athletes can enter as many events as they want. For kids who are still figuring out what they like, this is a good meet to experiment. For kids who are tired, help them make smart choices.
  • The atmosphere is great for first-timers. If you have athletes who have never competed before, this is an ideal introduction to a track meet.
Set expectations with athletes: “This meet is about having fun, trying events, and learning how a track meet works. Every one of you will run well. Cheer for your teammates no matter what.”

Logistics & Site Layout

The one thing that catches teams off guard at this venue.

  • The field events and running events are on opposite sides of a parking lot. They’re close, but athletes need to cross to get between them.
  • Recruit 1–2 parent volunteers specifically to help shuttle athletes between the field event area and the track. This is the most useful job at this meet — someone needs to keep an eye on which events are about to start and make sure the right kids are in the right place.
  • Athletes competing in both field events and running events need to know their schedule and be ready to move quickly. Brief them before the meet starts.
  • The meet moves faster than you expect — it was scheduled for 2 hours and ran closer to 1. If an athlete is hanging out watching instead of warming up, they may miss their event before they realize it.
Volunteer assignment tip: Give your parking lot shepherds a list of which athletes have events on both sides. They’ll know exactly who to watch for when the schedule jumps ahead.

Pre-Meet Athlete Briefing

Run through this with the full group before events start. Keep it quick — 5 minutes max.

💡
Tie your shoes — double-knot them

This is not a joke. We had an athlete lose a shoe mid-race in the 100m. It was hilarious in hindsight, and a real bummer in the moment. Check your laces before every event. Double-knot. No exceptions.

🏠
Know where you need to be

Field events and the track are across a parking lot from each other. If your next event is on the other side, start walking before the previous one finishes. Don’t wait to be told — know your schedule.

🏁
Cheer for your team

When you’re not competing, watch your teammates and cheer. A good crowd makes a real difference. This is a team sport even in individual events.

Teaching Moment: Staggered Starts & Relay Cut-In

Cover this with relay athletes before the meet, ideally at the last practice. These concepts confuse kids every year.

Why the Start Looks Uneven

  • In races run around a curve (like the 200m and relays), the outside lanes travel a longer distance to complete the same arc as the inside lane.
  • To make it fair, athletes in outer lanes start further ahead. This is the stagger. Everyone runs the same total distance — it just doesn’t look that way at the start.
  • Reassure athletes: “If the runner next to you appears to be ahead, that doesn’t mean they’re winning. You’ll find out where everyone actually stands once you cut in.”

What “Cutting In” Means

  • After the stagger is used up, there is a designated break line where all runners may leave their lanes and move to the inside.
  • The runner who reaches the break line first has the right-of-way to the inside lane. Athletes should not cut in aggressively — check that there’s room before moving in.
  • Once everyone has cut in, the order on the track reflects who is actually winning. This is where athletes often get confused and think they’re losing when they’re not — or vice versa.

Relay Cut-In: What Athletes Need to Know

Each runner starts in their assigned lane. The stagger means outer lanes start ahead of inner lanes. This is intentional and correct.
After completing the first curve (for most relays), runners reach the break line. Everyone moves to the inside lane in the order they arrive — do not cut in front of another runner if they’re already there.
Now the race is in a single-file group on the inside. The order of runners here is the actual race order. Athletes should now know if they are ahead, behind, or racing for position.
The baton exchange is timed to this new order. Incoming and outgoing runners need to be on the same page about where the exchange zone starts and how to call “stick!”
Common mistake: After cut-in, an athlete who started in lane 4 sees the lane 1 runner right next to them and panics thinking they’re getting passed — when actually both runners are right where they should be. Walk through this with athletes before the race so they aren’t surprised.

📢 What to Communicate to Parents

  • It’s a fast meet. It was scheduled for 2 hours and finished in about 1. Don’t show up late expecting to catch your athlete — their event may already be done.
  • No entry requirements. Athletes can do as many events as they want. If your athlete wants to try something new, this is the right meet for that.
  • Parking lot crossing. The field event area and the track are on opposite sides of a parking lot. Stay aware of where your athlete is so they don’t miss an event getting back to the track.
  • The vibe is great. This is one of the most fun, low-key meets of the season. Bring a lawn chair, cheer for everyone, and enjoy it.
  • Shoes. Make sure your athlete’s shoes are tied and double-knotted before every event. (Trust us on this one.)
